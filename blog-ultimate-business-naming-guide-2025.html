Why the name matters

A memorable name builds trust, boosts word‑of‑mouth, and scales across web, social, and ads without breaking. Pair it with a story and consistent visuals to accelerate recall.

7 name types with quick examples

Descriptive

Explains exactly what you do (e.g., General Motors, American Airlines).

Invented

Made‑up for branding power (e.g., Google, Kodak).

Metaphorical

Symbol/story‑driven (e.g., Apple, Nike).

Acronym

Initials of longer phrases (e.g., IBM, KFC).

Founder

Built from people’s names (e.g., Ford, J&J).

Geographic

Tied to a place (e.g., Southwest, Bank of America).

Compound

Two words fused (e.g., Facebook, Microsoft).

Naming formulas by industry

Tip: Mix one “what” word + one “feel” word for brandable blends.

Tech startups

  • Tech + Action: DataFlow, CloudSync, CodeStream
  • Future vibe: QuantumLeap, NexusPoint, VelocityLabs

Cafes & restaurants

  • Ingredient + Place: Bean Station, Roast Republic
  • Experience + Food: The Daily Grind, Brew & Bite

Consulting

  • Action + Result: StrategySync, ConsultCore
  • Trust + Direction: Compass Advisory, Summit Partners

Fashion & beauty

  • Style + Emotion: ChicWave, GlamCore
  • Modern + Minimal: Aura, Essence, Vogueworks

5‑step naming process

  1. Define audience, personality, vision.
  2. Research competitors and language patterns.
  3. Brainstorm associations, metaphors, portmanteaus.
  4. Filter with HEART: Heart, Easy, Available, Relevant, Timeless.
  5. Validate with 5 users + domain and trademark checks.

Domains, SEO, and legal

  • Prefer .com; .io/.co also fine for startups.
  • Short, hyphen‑free, human‑readable URLs help recall.
  • Search official trademark databases (USPTO/WIPO) before launch.

Psychology of memorable names

  • Hard consonants (K/G/D) feel strong and technical.
  • Soft sounds (L/M/N) feel friendly and service‑oriented.
  • 1–2 syllables are easiest to recall and type.

Common mistakes

  • Too long or hard to spell
  • Overly generic or trend‑chasing
  • No domain/trademark check
  • Names that limit future products
